# Reflection on AI and Security

## Connecting the Topics

While learning about AI and cybersecurity foundations, I noticed that many topics are connected through communication, trust, and workflow design rather than only technical systems.

For example:

- prompt engineering depends on providing clear context and constraints,
- phishing attacks often exploit urgency and familiarity,
- and responsible AI focuses on trust, transparency, and human oversight.

This made me realize that technology adoption is often closely connected to human behavior and organizational processes.

## AI as a Support Tool

One thing I find interesting is that many AI systems are not designed to fully replace people, but to support decision-making, communication, and operational efficiency.

However, this also creates new risks if users:

- overtrust AI-generated output,
- skip verification steps,
- or rely on automation without understanding limitations.

Because of this, human review and clear workflows still remain important.

## Security and Everyday Workflows

Learning about phishing also changed how I think about everyday digital workflows.

Many attacks do not appear obviously malicious. Instead, they imitate:

- delivery notifications,
- internal company messages,
- password reset requests,
- or customer communication.

This showed me that cybersecurity awareness is not only for technical specialists, but also important for general digital work environments.

## Additional Thought After Further Reading

After reading more about AI and cybersecurity, I noticed that AI is increasingly being used by both attackers and defenders.

For example:

- attackers may use AI to generate more convincing phishing messages,
- while security teams may use AI for anomaly detection, monitoring, and faster threat analysis.

This made me realize that AI can both improve productivity and expand the attack surface at the same time.

Another point I found interesting is that even with advanced AI systems, human judgment and verification still remain important.

Technology may support faster decision-making, but organizations still need:

- clear workflows,
- human oversight,
- and responsible governance practices.

I also noticed that many discussions around cybersecurity are not only about technical tools, but also about trust, communication, operational processes, and how people interact with technology in daily workflows.

## Reflection

So far, one of my biggest takeaways is that AI and cybersecurity are not isolated technical topics.

They are also connected to:

- communication,
- operations,
- governance,
- user behavior,
- and organizational trust.

This is one reason I wanted to organize my learning journey in a more structured way through this repository.
